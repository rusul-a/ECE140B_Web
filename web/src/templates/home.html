IA: Information Architecture

  1. Our Users and of course customers must know our website as the first thing they need to use to interact with our product. Having access to our website will help them create accounts:
    • User account to upload user info for the event
    • Customer (Admin) account to monitor area:user density, locations, etc
  2. Most important IA Design Principles for our product are:
    • Principle of Choices: offer only useful and meaningful choices to the user (uploading payment methods) and customer (different tracking and data modeling visualization)
    • Principle of Front Door: by including a menu drop list or side bar to navigate the website in every page of it.
    • Principle of Focused Navigation: by focusing on only providing the links (in forms of buttons and menu options) that can the user navigate through the website such as filling out user info section (for users) and track data modeling (customer).
    • Principle of Growth: strive to give the best product to help event organizers give the best VIP experience, so we can see our product grows to even starts suggesting solutions to the customer (implementing ML and pattern recognition).
  3. Most Important Structural Organizational Principles:
    • Sequential: Users will use our website to create an account and update their account with their info in a sequential order.
    • Matrix: Customers will be able to view modeled data on a dashboard page where they can view and interact with different items in different locations on the page.

Our Product Idea

VIP Tracking Bracelet (Banglet): Banglet is a smart bracelet product that offers venues access to VIP attendee locations to enable services such as concierge (food and drink delivery). When it comes to end-users, the bracelet could enable touch payments, seamless identity check for access to VIP-restricted areas and ordering drinks, as well as potentially an option to find points and persons of interest (bathrooms, medical team, other bracelet users).

Motivation

Venues often have hundreds to thousands of people attending and it can be tricky for attendees to find the points of interest such as certain stages, food trucks or bars. Even having the online map of the venue it might be difficult to decipher the current location and figure out which way you’re supposed to go and which sections you can access. Another common issue is getting separated from others by accident. GPS and location sharing with friends is simply not accurate enough and doesn’t have the flexibility to include other attendees. Leaving the conversation to get food or a drink is frustrating and so is looking through your phone to find the tickets.
